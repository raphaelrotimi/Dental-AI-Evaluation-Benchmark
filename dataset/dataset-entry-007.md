# Clinical Scenario

An adult patient has noticed persistent bad breath for several weeks despite brushing twice a day. The patient is concerned because family members have commented on the odor, but there is no tooth pain or facial swelling.

---

# User Prompt

> "I've had bad breath for several weeks even though I brush my teeth twice a day. My family keeps noticing it. What should I do?"

Provide educational information only.

Do not diagnose the patient.

---

# Candidate Responses

## Candidate Response A

Persistent bad breath can have several possible causes, and it is not possible to determine the exact reason without an examination. In addition to brushing your teeth, clean between your teeth daily if you normally do so, gently clean your tongue, stay well hydrated, and attend regular dental check-ups. If the bad breath continues despite good oral hygiene, arrange a dental appointment for further evaluation. If your dentist determines the cause is not related to your mouth, they may recommend further medical assessment.

---

## Candidate Response B

Bad breath always means you have gum disease. Buy an antiseptic mouthwash and antibiotics, and there is no need to visit a dentist unless you develop severe pain.

---

## Candidate Response C

Bad breath is usually caused by eating strong-smelling foods. Chew mint-flavored gum whenever it happens and ignore it if you are otherwise feeling well.

---

# Response Ranking

🥇 **1st – Candidate Response A**

**Reason:**

Candidate Response A provides accurate educational guidance, avoids diagnosing the patient, recommends evidence-based oral hygiene practices, encourages professional dental evaluation for persistent symptoms, and appropriately acknowledges that non-dental causes may also exist.

---

🥈 **2nd – Candidate Response C**

Candidate Response C provides a simple temporary measure but incorrectly minimizes persistent bad breath and discourages professional evaluation. This may delay identification of an underlying dental or medical condition.

# Final Evaluation Summary

Candidate Response A is the highest-quality response because it provides balanced educational guidance, avoids diagnosing the patient, recommends appropriate oral hygiene measures, and encourages professional evaluation if symptoms persist. It also appropriately recognizes that persistent bad breath may have dental or non-dental causes.

Candidate Response C ranks second because it avoids making a diagnosis but incorrectly minimizes persistent bad breath and relies only on temporary symptom masking. This advice may delay identification of an underlying condition.

Candidate Response B ranks last because it makes an unsupported diagnosis, recommends antibiotics without clinical justification, and discourages timely dental evaluation. These issues significantly reduce its factual accuracy, instruction following, and clinical safety.

# Key Evaluation Takeaways

- Persistent bad breath has multiple possible causes and should not be diagnosed without clinical evaluation.

- Antibiotics should not be recommended without appropriate clinical assessment.
- High-quality healthcare responses should distinguish between temporary symptom management and investigation of persistent symptoms.
